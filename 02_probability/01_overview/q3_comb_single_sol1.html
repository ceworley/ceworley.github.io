This scenario describes a combinations problem (order of selection does not matter). We are considering the subsets of size 5 from a set of size 11.

\[\begin{aligned} {_nC_r} &= \frac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!} \\\\ n &= 11 \\\\ r &= 5 \\\\ {_{11}C_{5}} &= \frac{11!}{(11-5)!\cdot 5! } \\\\ &= \frac{11!}{6! \cdot 5!} \\\\ &= \frac{ 11\cdot10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7}{ 5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1} \\\\ &= 462 \end{aligned}\]

Remember, we care about combinations because they represent all the ways we can select \(r\) 1s and \(n-r\) 0s. So, in this case, \({_{11}C_{5}} = 462\) tells us there are 462 ways of selecting 6 0s and 5 1s. (Think of the 1s as the toppings that are selected and 0s as the toppings NOT selected.)
