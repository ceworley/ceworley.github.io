This scenario describes a combinations problem (order of selection does not matter). We are considering the subsets of size 4 from a set of size 22.

\[\begin{aligned} {_nC_r} &= \frac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!} \\\\ n &= 22 \\\\ r &= 4 \\\\ {_{22}C_{4}} &= \frac{22!}{(22-4)!\cdot 4! } \\\\ &= \frac{22!}{18! \cdot 4!} \\\\ &= \frac{ 22\cdot21\cdot20\cdot19}{ 4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1} \\\\ &= 7315 \end{aligned}\]

Remember, we care about combinations because they represent all the ways we can select \(r\) 1s and \(n-r\) 0s. So, in this case, \({_{22}C_{4}} = 7315\) tells us there are 7315 ways of selecting 18 0s and 4 1s. (Think of the 1s as the toppings that are selected and 0s as the toppings NOT selected.)
